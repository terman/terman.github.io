Wengen Wanderwegs — Summer 2016

Bachalpsee is small alpine lake perched high above Grindlewald. A gondola ride makes short work of the climb, so after a 30-min walk many hundreds of visitors are soaking in this great view. So it takes a little doing to find a shot that makes it look like I have it mostly to myself :)
Happily there's an alpine trail that leads away from the crowds and offers spectacular views of Grindlewald and the big three. Well, the big two since from this angle Mönch is hidden behind the Eiger.
Wengen (where I'm staying) is a resort village nestled comfortably on a plateau some 1500' above the floor of the Lauterbrunnen valley, part of what the Swiss travel folks call "the Top of Europe". They've been welcoming guests since the 1850's for hiking in the summer and skiing in the winter.
I think much of Wegen's charm is that it's car-free and can only be reached by the cog railway connecting Lauterbrunnen (and it's very large parking garage!) to Kleine Scheidegg. It's neat having the pedestrians rule the roost for a change!
I'm guessing that the most popular walk in Switzerland is the route from Männlichen (reached by cable car from Wengen) to Kleine Scheidegg. Why so popular? You spend the entire walk (60 mins if you're taking your time) looking at the big three: left-to-right we have the majestic Eiger, Mönch, and Jungfrau. Grabbing our German dictionary, we learn that the monk is interposing himself between the ogre and the virgin.
This is the hugely busy Kleine Scheidegg train station where many 1000's of toursists each day switch to the special cog trains to the Jungfraujoch, Europe's highest train station at 11,332'. The trains reach the top via a 5.6 mile tunnel through Eiger and Monch, a marvel of engineering completed in 1912. For about $200, you too can be part of the throng and visit the tourist wonderland they've built on a col under the Jungfrau peak. My preference is for a nice quiet alpine trail, but to each their own.
The Walenpfad (wales path?) is a classic alpine hike from Bannalp to Bruni with the luxurious amenity of cable cars at both ends! Here's the view from top of starting lift. The trail wends it way left-to-right above an alpine lake then across the grassy ridge you see on the far right.
And here's the view from the ridge, looking back towards the starting point. Despite the help from the lift, there's still 2000' of uphill to be conquered.
The trails are easy to follow and, assuming the cows haven't trammeled them into mud, quite well maintained.
Topping each ridge reveals another, higher ridge to cross. I thought this one was especially handsome, looking almost furry with its lush covering of foliage.
And then I discovered the rail runs right along the knife edge!
At last, the highest elevation on this trail! Signage is terrific and, amazingly, my hiking times closely match those listed on the signs. A great spot for lunch... What about the view? Click here for the panorama.
Several hours later the end is in sight: the small cluster of buildings in the upper left. Very civilized to have a mountain restaurant where I can get my celebratory drink of Apfel Schorle (a beer would go right to my head at this point!).
Water is big in Switerland -- glaciers and rivers are resposible for much of the justifiably famous scenery. Of course, Swiss rivers aren't allowed to just plunge willy-nilly wherever they wish. There's a lot of engineering to control their flow, generate electricity, etc. Here's a dam (associated at one time with a 19th century mill I think) with a fish ladder on the left. I'm not sure I understand yet how the fish know to use the ladder, but I'm sure Swiss fish figure it out ;)
No respectable chalet is without its flowers, mostly shades of red naturally. Friendly pottery gnomes are optional. If you want to make a fortune, corner the red geranium market here in Switzerland -- every window box on every chalet (or so it seems) uses them.
If the valley is wide enough, you'll see many fields used as pasture. In the summer when the cows are in the high alps (alp refers to both the mountains and the high meadows on their upper slopes) the fields are used to raise grass as fodder. The meadows always look well groomed as the grass is cut frequently. My yard should look so good!
A cow wash? Another business idea: cow detailing for the Swiss farmer looking to win another cow-bell prize.
Meadows, chalets, snow covered peaks... this must be Switzerland! In this case a view up the Lauterbrunnen valley on the way up to the Leiterhorn. Wengen is the town on the left, perched 1500' above the floor of the valley. A light rain is keeping the scenery lush and green, but I'm momentarily dry on a bench nestled under a fir. I'm listening to the patter of the rain and church bells accompanied by distant cow bells. (Click here to hear them for yourself!)
Wengen is car-free and can only be reached by cog railway. The trains run frequently from 5a to 1a and connect seamlessly with the extensive Swiss transporation system. I've found the trains and buses to be super-reliable -- I think they could make all of Switzerland car-free and folks would still be able function.
