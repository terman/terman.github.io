Canyons! — Summer 2016

You'll be shocked to learn that what one sees at Arches National Park is... arches. They came in all sizes and ages. This particularly study example is Turret Arch.
I'm getting a good grasp for the color "sage green."
Arches has millions of visitors each year, so it takes some effort to frame and time a shot to make it look like you have the place to yourself. This is Broken Arch.
This is Landscape Arch, which has the longest span of any arch in the park. It seems impossibly thin at places, making you wonder how much longer it will be with us.
Capitol Reef National Park's main attraction is the Waterpocket Fold, a 100-mile long wrinkle in the earth.
There are signs at each trailhead explaining that there's a $300 fine for writing grafitti. Petroglyphs are okay however, but the window of opportunity for that closed 1000 years ago.
If you like hiking arid canyons, I recommend Capitol Gorge, the home of the petroglyph shown above. Here I've climbed to the top of the gorge to see a genuine water pocket (which turns out to be a depression in the rock holding water, vastly oversold as a tourist destination).
Today we traveled Utah's scenic highway 12 in a 150-mile trip through some of the most rugged and remote landscape in the lower 48. The day started by catching the sunrise at Bryce Canyon with the strong eastern light adding even more drama to the view.
Then we visited some teenage hoodoos hanging out together away from their parents.
It took the Civilian Conservation Core many years to find a path across the rugged canyonlands of the Grand Staircase. Until Highway 12 was completed, the few remote communities had to be served by mule train. Crossing the mountain ridges in the distance (today's destination) must have seemed easy by comparison.
Later in the day, we had a chance to hike up the Lower Calf Creek Falls trail, an interesting mix of desert landscape and lush creek-fed foliage along the canyon bottom.
Bryce Canyon is hoodoo central! To the Paiute the hoodoos are the "Legend People," turned to stone by their trickster god Coyote as punishment for their bad deeds. Formed in their rank and file, they remind me of a giant version of the burried terracotta army of Xian.
To a geologist, hoodoos are fins of sandstone protected from erosion by a stripe of tougher rock. Once exposed, cracks form and are expanded by the action of water and ice, eventually separating the fin into a sequence of separate columns. The totem-pole profile comes from softer layers eroding faster than harder layers.
Hoodoos aren't forever, eventually wearing away to mere nubs and eventually a fine, red sand.

Zion owes its existence to the Virgin River, which you see here emerging from the Narrows into the wider lower canyon. We're there bright and early, with the east light creating a marvelous lightscape on the western canyon walls.
You can actually hike quite far up into the Narrows. Well, hike isn't quite the right word: it's more splashing, wading, or swimming in cold water. Next time we'll rent the right shoes and give it a try!j
